## What to Expect

Swimming in a cenote is one of the most magical experiences in Mexico. These natural sinkholes, formed in the Yucatan's limestone bedrock, are filled with crystal-clear freshwater that stays a refreshing 24-26 degrees Celsius year-round. Some cenotes are open to the sky like natural swimming pools, while others are partially or fully enclosed caves with stalactites, tree roots descending from above, and ethereal beams of light piercing through openings.

The water clarity in cenotes is extraordinary -- visibility can exceed 30 meters in cave cenotes. The experience ranges from floating in a sun-drenched pool surrounded by jungle to swimming through underground caverns lit by shafts of sunlight.

## Types of Cenote Experiences

### Open-Air Cenotes
Best for families and casual swimmers. Examples include Cenote Azul near Playa del Carmen and Cenote Zaci in Valladolid. These feel like natural swimming pools surrounded by jungle or rock walls.

### Semi-Open Cenotes
The most photogenic, with partial rock overhangs creating dramatic light effects. Ik Kil near [Chichen Itza](/destinations/chichen-itza/) and Suytun near Valladolid are classic examples, with vines and roots hanging from above.

### Cave Cenotes
Underground chambers with stalactites and crystal-clear water. Gran Cenote near [Tulum](/cities/tulum/) and Cenote Samula near Valladolid offer this experience. Snorkeling here reveals underwater formations.

### Cenote Diving
For certified divers, cenotes like Dos Ojos and Angelita offer access to extensive underwater cave systems. This requires advanced certification and should only be done with qualified guides.

## Tips for Cenote Swimming

- **Shower before entering** -- Required at most cenotes to protect the water
- **No chemical sunscreen** -- Biodegradable sunscreen only, or ideally none at all. The minerals in sunscreen damage the cenote ecosystem.
- **Life jackets** -- Required at many cenotes, especially for non-swimmers. Usually provided.
- **Waterproof camera** -- Essential for capturing the incredible underwater scenery
- **Snorkel gear** -- Bring your own or rent at the cenote. A mask and snorkel greatly enhance the experience in cave cenotes.
- **Water shoes** -- Helpful for rocky entries and slippery steps
- **Arrive early** -- Popular cenotes near Tulum and Chichen Itza get crowded by mid-morning

## Cost

Entry fees range from 100-500 MXN depending on the cenote. More developed cenotes with facilities (lockers, showers, restaurants) charge more. Life jacket and locker rental may be extra (30-50 MXN each).
